The Worlds Surveys Don’t See

A physics-validated synthetic exoplanet population for measuring what transit surveys and future observatories miss

Populations 120,000 systems, 263,367 planets Instruments TESS · Kepler · JWST · Roman · HWO Status working prototype, v0.2

Abstract

Every exoplanet catalog is the output of a filter: survey geometry, instrument noise, and observing baselines decide which worlds become data points and which remain invisible. exoverse inverts the problem. Instead of starting from what was detected, it generates the full spectrum of plausible planetary systems bottom-up — from stellar physics and measured occurrence statistics — then observes each world with modeled instruments, real and future. The gap between what exists in the synthetic universe and what its instruments recover is a direct measurement of survey selection effects. In a 100,000-system population we find that 99% of habitable-zone rocky planets are invisible to every transit survey modeled, purely by geometry. A companion 20,000-system solar-neighborhood population gives the Habitable Worlds Observatory a realistic target field: it images 522 planets, including 44 habitable-zone Earth-size candidates — which scales to ≈22 exo-Earths for the real solar neighborhood, closely matching the mission’s Astro2020 design goal of 25, without being tuned to it.

1Motivation

Nearly 6,000 exoplanets are confirmed, and most were found the same way: a planet crossed the face of its star and dimmed it for a few hours. The transit method is spectacularly productive and spectacularly biased. It requires an orbital plane aligned with our line of sight — a coin-flip a planet at 1 AU around a Sun-like star wins less than half a percent of the time — and it favors big planets on short orbits around quiet, bright stars. The catalog we reason from is not the population that exists; it is the population that geometry and noise let through.

Astronomers correct for this with completeness models fitted to each survey. exoverse takes the complementary route: build the underlying population first. If you can generate stellar systems that are physically self-consistent and statistically faithful to known occurrence rates, you can push them through modeled instruments and watch the selection function act on individual worlds — not as an abstract correction factor, but as a named planet, with a mass and a sky position, that a given telescope does or does not see.

2Goals and approach

The project has three standing goals:

  • Generate plausible planetary systems from physics upward — a Kroupa mass function for the stars, main-sequence scaling relations, occurrence-shaped periods and radii (including the Fulton radius valley), probabilistic mass–radius relations, orbital-stability screening, stellar noise, and atmosphere assignment.
  • Observe every world with modeled instruments spanning past, present, and future: Kepler (archival), TESS, JWST transit spectroscopy, ground-based 1-m surveys, the Roman Space Telescope’s bulge survey, and the Habitable Worlds Observatory’s coronagraphic imaging.
  • Validate the generator against reality — running real cataloged planets through the same physical-validity rules, and comparing the synthetic detectable population against the real detected one with the selection forward-modeled.

Three design commitments shape everything. First, plausibility is first-class output: every approximation carries a severity-graded flag. Hard physics violations (denser than pure iron, above the deuterium-burning mass limit, dynamically unstable pairs) are rejected and resampled; questionable-but-possible regimes are kept and labeled, because “plausible but uncertain” is exactly where the interesting science lives. Second, every world is reproducible: a system regenerates bit-for-bit from its random seed, so light curves are never stored — they are re-derived on demand. Third, the database is the product: 120,000 systems, each individually inspectable through a CLI and a web interface, with full metadata down to limb-darkening coefficients and cloud-suppression factors.

2.1  Two populations, two questions

Version 0.2 maintains two datasets. The main population — 100,000 systems with host stars out to 300 pc — answers transit-survey questions, where the observable volume is deep. But a deep volume starves a direct-imaging mission: only ~0.1% of its systems fall within the Habitable Worlds Observatory’s ~30 pc reach. So a second population caps host distance at 30 pc — 20,000 systems representing the solar neighborhood — generated by the same physics with a single rescaled distance draw, leaving every other property untouched.

3Validation against the real catalog

Before trusting conclusions from synthetic worlds, the generator is audited against 8,240 confirmed planets. The audit runs in both directions: real planets are pushed through exoverse’s physical-validity rules, and the synthetic Kepler-detectable population is compared against real transit discoveries.

No real planet violates a rule for a reason the generator can’t account for — the two nonzero rows are known catalog artifacts, and their rates are stable from the earlier 10,000-system run to 100,000. On the population side, the period distribution of synthetic Kepler-detectable planets tracks real discoveries closely (median log-period 0.98 vs 0.90), while the host-temperature comparison fails by construction — real surveys target Sun-like stars, whereas exoverse samples the true, M-dwarf-dominated mass function. That deliberate failure is the point: it demonstrates the selection function is being modeled rather than inherited.

4Findings

4.1  Scale makes rare regimes measurable

Growing the population tenfold (the first 10,000 worlds are preserved exactly — seeds are a stream) populated regimes that were statistical noise before: 671 ultra-short-period planets, 47 mega-Earths, 18 ultra-hot worlds, and 4 super-puffs now exist in queryable numbers. Detection counts scale linearly with population size, confirming the smaller run wasn’t a fluke, and the Kepler-detectable radius median refined from 0.34 to 0.32 dex — a sample-size sharpening, since the generation physics is unchanged.

4.2  Transit surveys are blind to 99% of habitable-zone rocky worlds

Figure 2 states the project’s central result at population scale. The transit method’s geometric filter removes 98.8% of habitable-zone rocky planets before noise or cadence even enter the picture, and instrument thresholds remove a further sliver. Statements like “transit probability is low” are textbook; what a synthetic population adds is the ability to hold the missing 16,970 worlds in a database — each with a name, a host star, an atmosphere class, and the specific reason it was missed.

4.3  An exo-Earth budget for the Habitable Worlds Observatory

HWO — NASA’s planned flagship for the 2040s — will not use transits at all. It will directly image reflected starlight, and its Astro2020 charge is to characterize at least 25 potentially habitable exo-Earths[6]. exoverse models it with a coronagraphic contrast floor of 3×10−11 that degrades photon-limited for hosts fainter than V≈7, working angles of 60–500 mas, and reflected-light contrast computed from a geometric albedo assigned by atmosphere class — Jupiter-like 0.50 down to airless 0.12, anchored on solar-system bodies.

In the 20,000-system neighborhood population, HWO images 522 planets. Crucially, 44 of them are exo-Earth candidates — habitable-zone planets of 0.8–1.4 Earth radii. Because the synthetic field holds roughly twice as many stars as the real solar neighborhood within 30 pc, the scaled prediction is ≈22 exo-Earths — landing within striking distance of the mission’s ≥25 goal from independent physics, with no tuning toward that number.

The loss accounting explains why M dwarfs — the most common stars — contribute nothing: their habitable zones are angularly too small to resolve and their photospheres too faint to post-process against, a conclusion that emerges here from the noise model rather than from an imposed target list. The atmosphere-class albedo also leaves a visible fingerprint: only 5 of 1,051 airless worlds are ever imaged, while cloudy secondary-atmosphere and steam worlds dominate the detections.

5Future directions

The findings above are demonstrations of an instrument — a population you can point at questions. Below are the directions where that instrument appears most valuable to the wider community, based on a survey of current literature.

5.1  Yield forecasting for missions now launching

ESA’s PLATO launches in late 2026, and yield studies built on population synthesis are already shaping its strategy — recent work forward-models ~13,000 expected detections across the Galactic thin disk, thick disk, and halo[1]. Roman’s bulge survey is predicted to find 60,000–200,000 transiting planets via pixel-level simulation[2]. exoverse’s contribution to this class of work is speed and inspectability: at ~2,000 fully-validated systems per second, survey trade studies (cadence, baseline, magnitude limits) can be re-run interactively, with every gained or lost detection traceable to a specific named world.

5.2  HWO target selection and design trades

The HWO community is actively assembling prioritized target lists — the 2025 Target Stars and Systems catalog[5], the ExEP Mission Star List[6], and new stellar-activity catalogs aimed squarely at reducing yield uncertainty. Published yield analyses identify the occurrence rate of Earth-like planets (η⊕) and exozodiacal dust as the dominant unknowns, and ask which design changes would protect the 25-exo-Earth goal[7]. A synthetic neighborhood population complements this directly: loss-mode budgets like Figure 3 quantify the marginal yield of a smaller inner working angle versus a deeper contrast floor — the exact trade Phase-A architecture studies must settle. Extending exoverse with exozodi levels and multi-visit scheduling would make those trades quantitative.

5.3  Demographic inference and η⊕

Occurrence-rate studies must invert the selection function to recover the true population from the observed one. A generative population where the truth is known by construction is the natural test harness for those inversion methods: inject a synthetic universe, apply a modeled survey, hand the “detections” to an occurrence-rate pipeline, and check whether it recovers the input. This closed-loop validation is difficult with real data — the answer is unknown — and is a standing gap the community fills with per-survey injection–recovery exercises that don’t transfer between instruments.

5.4  Training corpora for machine-learning detection

Deep-learning vetting models now routinely triage transit candidates at ~97% precision, and several groups report that models trained on combined real and synthetic light curves outperform those trained on real data alone[3] [4]. exoverse generates limb-darkened light curves with realistic stellar noise (granulation, spots, flares) and perfect ground-truth labels — including the hard negatives that matter: grazing transits, active hosts, and depths straddling detection thresholds. A pipeline exporting labeled light-curve corpora would be a small extension with a direct user base.

5.5  An open, explorable universe

Because every world is individually inspectable — with plausibility flags that say why a value is uncertain and citations for every approximation — the database doubles as a teaching instrument for selection effects, observational bias, and instrument design. Deploying the read-only web interface publicly, alongside downloadable populations, is the lowest-effort path to community use. On the modeling roadmap: N-body verification of tightly-packed systems, binary hosts, transit-timing variations, and radiative-transfer spectra to replace scale-height heuristics.
