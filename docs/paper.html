The Worlds Surveys Don’t See

A physics-validated synthetic exoplanet population for measuring what transit surveys and future observatories miss

Populations 1,007,083 systems, 2,212,734 planets Instruments TESS · Kepler · JWST · Roman · HWO Status working prototype, v0.3

Abstract

Every exoplanet catalog is the output of a filter: survey geometry, instrument noise, and observing baselines decide which worlds become data points and which remain invisible. exoverse inverts the problem. Instead of starting from what was detected, it generates the full spectrum of plausible planetary systems bottom-up — from stellar physics and measured occurrence statistics — then observes each world with modeled instruments, real and future. Version 0.3 makes the synthetic universe an explicit sample of the real one: distances follow the Galactic thin disk’s vertical structure with an absolute density normalization (~5.2 million systems truly exist within 300 pc), the main population grows to 1,000,000 systems (a 19% sample of that reality), and a companion population is a 1:1 analog of the actual solar neighborhood — 7,083 systems within 30 pc, so mission yields read as absolute predictions. Reflected-light albedos are now drawn per planet from temperature-dependent distributions spanning the measured range, from nearly-black hot Jupiters to Venus-bright cloud decks. In this universe, 99% of habitable-zone rocky planets remain invisible to every transit survey modeled, purely by geometry; the Habitable Worlds Observatory images 209 planets, including 19 habitable-zone Earth-size candidates — just below its Astro2020 goal of 25 — and its exo-Earth yield turns out to be limited almost entirely by inner working angle, not by contrast floor or host brightness: halving the 60 mas working angle quintuples the yield, while a 3× deeper floor gains nothing.

1Motivation

Nearly 6,000 exoplanets are confirmed, and most were found the same way: a planet crossed the face of its star and dimmed it for a few hours. The transit method is spectacularly productive and spectacularly biased. It requires an orbital plane aligned with our line of sight — a coin-flip a planet at 1 AU around a Sun-like star wins less than half a percent of the time — and it favors big planets on short orbits around quiet, bright stars. The catalog we reason from is not the population that exists; it is the population that geometry and noise let through.

Astronomers correct for this with completeness models fitted to each survey. exoverse takes the complementary route: build the underlying population first. If you can generate stellar systems that are physically self-consistent and statistically faithful to known occurrence rates, you can push them through modeled instruments and watch the selection function act on individual worlds — not as an abstract correction factor, but as a named planet, with a mass and a sky position, that a given telescope does or does not see.

2Goals and approach

The project has three standing goals:

  • Generate plausible planetary systems from physics upward — a Kroupa mass function for the stars, disk-structure distances normalized to the real local density, main-sequence scaling relations, occurrence-shaped periods and radii (including the Fulton radius valley), probabilistic mass–radius relations, orbital-stability screening, stellar noise, atmosphere assignment, and per-planet reflected-light albedos.
  • Observe every world with modeled instruments spanning past, present, and future: Kepler (archival), TESS, JWST transit spectroscopy, ground-based 1-m surveys, the Roman Space Telescope’s bulge survey, and the Habitable Worlds Observatory’s coronagraphic imaging.
  • Validate the generator against reality — running real cataloged planets through the same physical-validity rules, and comparing the synthetic detectable population against the real detected one with the selection forward-modeled.

Three design commitments shape everything. First, plausibility is first-class output: every approximation carries a severity-graded flag. Hard physics violations (denser than pure iron, above the deuterium-burning mass limit, dynamically unstable pairs) are rejected and resampled; questionable-but-possible regimes are kept and labeled, because “plausible but uncertain” is exactly where the interesting science lives. Second, every world is reproducible: a system regenerates bit-for-bit from its random seed, so light curves are never stored — they are re-derived on demand. Third, the database is the product: over a million systems, each individually inspectable through a CLI and a web interface, with full metadata down to limb-darkening coefficients, per-planet albedos, and cloud-suppression factors.

2.1  Two populations, calibrated to the real sky

How many systems should a 300 pc population contain? Anchoring the local density on the RECONS 10 pc census and Gaia’s Catalogue of Nearby Stars (0.065 main-sequence systems per pc³) and letting density fall off vertically with the thin disk’s ~300 pc scale height, the answer is ≈5.2 million — the earlier 100,000-system run sampled just 2% of them. Version 0.3 therefore (a) replaces the uniform-density distance draw with the disk-structure one, (b) grows the main population tenfold to 1,000,000 systems — a 19% random sample of reality, and a bit-exact prefix of a future full 5.2-million run, since seeds form a stream — and (c) reports in every stats output what fraction of reality a population represents.

The companion population goes further: within 30 pc the same density model expects 7,083 systems, so the neighborhood dataset is generated at exactly that count. It is a 1:1 analog of the real solar neighborhood — when the modeled Habitable Worlds Observatory images 19 exo-Earth candidates there, that number is an absolute yield estimate, no scaling required.

3Validation against the real catalog

Before trusting conclusions from synthetic worlds, the generator is audited against 8,240 confirmed planets. The audit runs in both directions: real planets are pushed through exoverse’s physical-validity rules, and the synthetic Kepler-detectable population is compared against real transit discoveries.

No real planet violates a rule for a reason the generator can’t account for — the two nonzero rows are known catalog artifacts, and their rates are stable from the 10,000-system era through 1,000,000. On the population side, the period distribution of 25,092 synthetic Kepler-detectable planets tracks real discoveries closely (median log-period 0.98 vs 0.90, KS D = 0.07), while the host-temperature comparison fails by construction (D = 0.58) — real surveys target Sun-like stars, whereas exoverse samples the true, M-dwarf-dominated mass function. That deliberate failure is the point: it demonstrates the selection function is being modeled rather than inherited. One statistical note at this scale: with 25,000 detections, KS p-values collapse to zero for even trivial differences, so the D statistic — not p — is the meaningful health metric.

4Findings

4.1  Scale makes rare regimes measurable

At a million systems, regimes that were statistical noise at 10,000 are now populations in their own right: 6,787 ultra-short-period planets, 390 mega-Earths, 188 ultra-hot worlds, and 37 super-puffs exist in queryable numbers, each with named, inspectable members. Detection counts continue to scale linearly with population size, and the Kepler-detectable radius median sits at 0.31 dex on a 25,092-planet detected sample. (The v0.3 physics changes — disk-structure distances and per-planet albedos — regenerated every world; within v0.3, the million systems are a bit-exact prefix of any future, larger run.)

4.2  Transit surveys are blind to 99% of habitable-zone rocky worlds

Figure 2 states the project’s central result at population scale. The transit method’s geometric filter removes 99.0% of habitable-zone rocky planets before noise or cadence even enter the picture, and instrument thresholds remove a further sliver. Statements like “transit probability is low” are textbook; what a synthetic population adds is the ability to hold the missing 170,000 worlds in a database — each with a name, a host star, an atmosphere class, and the specific reason it was missed. And because the population is now normalized to the real stellar density, the statement extends to the sky itself: within 300 pc of the Sun, the model implies roughly 900,000 habitable-zone rocky planets exist, of which transit geometry offers up fewer than 9,000.

4.3  An exo-Earth budget for the Habitable Worlds Observatory

HWO — NASA’s planned flagship for the 2040s — will not use transits at all. It will directly image reflected starlight, and its Astro2020 charge is to characterize at least 25 potentially habitable exo-Earths[6]. exoverse models it with a coronagraphic contrast floor of 3×10−11 that degrades photon-limited for hosts fainter than V≈7, working angles of 60–500 mas, and reflected-light contrast computed from each planet’s own geometric albedo. Where v0.2 assigned one fixed albedo per atmosphere class, v0.3 draws Ag per planet from temperature-dependent distributions spanning the measured range: clear alkali-absorbing hot Jupiters are nearly black (the Kepler/CHEOPS population sits at 0.03–0.11, TrES-2b below 0.04), cold ammonia-cloud giants reflect ~0.5, secondary atmospheres center on Earth’s re-measured 0.24 with a 22% Venus-like branch near 0.65, and cold airless worlds get an icy-bright branch (Europa-class). The classic yield-study convention is a flat 0.2 for everything; the real range spans a factor of ~20.

In the 7,083-system population — a 1:1 analog of the real solar neighborhood — HWO images 209 planets. Crucially, 19 of them are exo-Earth candidates — habitable-zone planets of 0.8–1.4 Earth radii. Because the population is no longer an arbitrary sample size, that count needs no scaling: it is the model’s yield estimate, and it lands just below the mission’s ≥25 goal from independent physics, with no tuning toward that number. The shortfall itself is informative, as §4.4 shows: the yield is sitting on the steep part of the inner-working-angle curve.

The loss accounting explains why M dwarfs — the most common stars — contribute nothing: their habitable zones are angularly too small to resolve and their photospheres too faint to post-process against, a conclusion that emerges here from the noise model rather than from an imposed target list. The per-planet albedo leaves its own fingerprint: only 3 of 400 airless worlds are ever imaged, while cloudy steam and secondary-atmosphere worlds dominate the detections (183 of 209) — and Venus-branch cloud decks are over-represented among the imaged secondaries (27%, against 22% in the population), the reflected-light analog of a selection effect.

4.4  The exo-Earth yield is an inner-working-angle problem

Because every planet’s contrast and separation are stored, HWO’s three design levers — inner working angle, post-processed contrast floor, and host-brightness envelope — can be swept over the same 1:1 neighborhood. The result is one-sided: over the plausible trade space, the yield responds only to the working angle. Deepening the contrast floor threefold (3×10−11 → 1×10−11) adds zero candidates; relaxing the host limit from V < 11 to 12.5 adds zero (the fainter hosts’ habitable zones are unresolvable anyway — those two loss modes are the same stars); and the detected planets clear the floor by a median factor of 12. But every milliarcsecond of working angle opens new K-dwarf habitable zones: 60 → 45 mas takes the yield from 19 to 48, and 30 mas reaches 99.

The same sweep quantifies the albedo question this version set out to test: replacing every drawn albedo with the conventional flat Ag = 0.2 changes the exo-Earth count by zero at every working angle, and the total imaged count by 1% (207 vs 209). Within this model’s envelope, reflected-light detectability is geometry-limited for any Ag ≳ 0.15 — albedo uncertainty instead moves each target’s contrast margin, i.e. how characterizable it is once found. The widened albedo range matters most at the bright end (Venus-branch worlds at 3–4× the contrast of an Earth twin) and for the darkest hot giants, which drop out entirely.

4.5  What the real sky has that the null universe lacks

exoverse deliberately omits formation and migration physics: radii are drawn independently within a system, spacings are shaped only by stability, and nothing depletes the hot-Neptune desert. That makes the synthetic detected population a null model — identical selection, no formation memory — so differences from the real detected population isolate astrophysics that selection cannot explain. Two of these are known results reproduced against a cleaner null — that is the tool working as intended. The third is the outside-the-box dividend: because the null has no formation correlations, it shows the “Kepler dichotomy” and “peas-in-a-pod” are not independent observations — a population with correlated sizes and no extra inclination component would already shift the singles ratio in the observed direction. Disentangling the two requires exactly the kind of joint forward model this population provides.

5Future directions

The findings above are demonstrations of an instrument — a population you can point at questions. Below are the directions where that instrument appears most valuable to the wider community, based on a survey of current literature.

5.1  Yield forecasting for missions now launching

ESA’s PLATO launches in late 2026, and yield studies built on population synthesis are already shaping its strategy — recent work forward-models ~13,000 expected detections across the Galactic thin disk, thick disk, and halo[1]. Roman’s bulge survey is predicted to find 60,000–200,000 transiting planets via pixel-level simulation[2]. exoverse’s contribution to this class of work is speed and inspectability: at ~2,000 fully-validated systems per second, survey trade studies (cadence, baseline, magnitude limits) can be re-run interactively, with every gained or lost detection traceable to a specific named world.

5.2  HWO target selection and design trades

The HWO community is actively assembling prioritized target lists — the 2025 Target Stars and Systems catalog[5], the ExEP Mission Star List[6], and new stellar-activity catalogs aimed squarely at reducing yield uncertainty. Published yield analyses identify the occurrence rate of Earth-like planets (η⊕) and exozodiacal dust as the dominant unknowns, and ask which design changes would protect the 25-exo-Earth goal[7]. A synthetic neighborhood population complements this directly — and §4.4 now delivers the first such trade quantitatively: over this model’s envelope the exo-Earth yield responds only to inner working angle, crossing the 25-planet goal between 60 and 45 mas. The natural next refinements are an integration-time budget (where albedo and contrast margin buy survey efficiency rather than detectability), exozodi levels, and multi-visit scheduling — each one grafts onto the same stored per-planet contrasts and separations.

5.3  Demographic inference and η⊕

Occurrence-rate studies must invert the selection function to recover the true population from the observed one. A generative population where the truth is known by construction is the natural test harness for those inversion methods: inject a synthetic universe, apply a modeled survey, hand the “detections” to an occurrence-rate pipeline, and check whether it recovers the input. This closed-loop validation is difficult with real data — the answer is unknown — and is a standing gap the community fills with per-survey injection–recovery exercises that don’t transfer between instruments. The absolute density normalization added in v0.3 strengthens this use: occurrence rates inferred from the modeled surveys can now be compared against the generator’s truth in physical units (planets per star, planets per pc³), not just in shape. The architecture null model of §4.5 extends the same idea to system-level statistics — any inference method that claims to measure mutual inclinations or intra-system correlations can be handed the null universe first, where the right answer is “none.”

5.4  Training corpora for machine-learning detection

Deep-learning vetting models now routinely triage transit candidates at ~97% precision, and several groups report that models trained on combined real and synthetic light curves outperform those trained on real data alone[3] [4]. exoverse generates limb-darkened light curves with realistic stellar noise (granulation, spots, flares) and perfect ground-truth labels — including the hard negatives that matter: grazing transits, active hosts, and depths straddling detection thresholds. A pipeline exporting labeled light-curve corpora would be a small extension with a direct user base.

5.5  An open, explorable universe

Because every world is individually inspectable — with plausibility flags that say why a value is uncertain and citations for every approximation — the database doubles as a teaching instrument for selection effects, observational bias, and instrument design. Deploying the read-only web interface publicly, alongside downloadable populations, is the lowest-effort path to community use. On the modeling roadmap: N-body verification of tightly-packed systems, binary hosts, transit-timing variations, and radiative-transfer spectra to replace scale-height heuristics.
